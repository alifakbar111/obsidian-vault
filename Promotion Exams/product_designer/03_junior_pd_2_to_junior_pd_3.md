# Promotion Exam: Junior Product Designer 2 → Junior Product Designer 3

**Track:** Product Designer (Specialist — visual / interaction / UX foundations)
**Format:** 100 multiple-choice questions
**Time:** 150 minutes
**Passing score:** 89 / 100

**Scope.** Junior Product Designer 3 is the top of the specialist track before convergence into the generalist senior level. A Junior PD 3 owns the design of a medium feature area with minimal supervision, drives research, designs complex interactions and flows, contributes substantially to the design system, works fluently with data, collaborates across functions as a near-peer, and shows early product-design judgment. This exam tests that depth: advanced interaction and information design, research that shapes decisions, data-informed design, complex responsive and systems work, content design, and the influence and product thinking a Junior 3 must demonstrate.

**Reminder.** This is the last specialist-only product-design exam. The next exam (Junior 3 → Senior 1) is the convergence point and tests whether the candidate has broadened from a specialist (leaning visual, or UX, or interaction) into a generalist product designer who does research, interaction, visual, and systems work, thinks about product strategy and the business, and collaborates with engineering with real technical literacy. A candidate who aces this but cannot connect design to business outcomes or work fluently with engineers is not ready for the next gate.

## Section A — Advanced Interaction and Information Design (Q1–Q14)

**1.** Designing a complex, multi-step flow (e.g., onboarding, checkout):
A) One long screen
B) Requires structuring steps to minimize effort and abandonment — clear progress, sensible grouping, the ability to go back, and graceful handling of errors mid-flow
C) Is the engineer's job
D) Needs no special thought

**2.** Information architecture for a growing product:
A) Add new features wherever there is space
B) Requires deliberate structure — as products grow, unmanaged IA becomes a maze; a Junior 3 thinks about how new features fit the overall organization
C) Is fixed once and never revisited
D) Is the PM's job

**3.** Navigation at scale (many sections and features):
A) Put everything in one menu
B) Requires prioritization and structure — surfacing the important, grouping logically, and not overwhelming users as the product grows
C) Is unsolvable
D) Is purely visual

**4.** Designing for different levels of user expertise:
A) Design for one level only
B) Accommodate the journey — approachable for newcomers while offering efficiency (shortcuts, density options) for power users, without compromising either
C) Only power users matter
D) Only novices matter

**5.** A design pattern that tests well in one context:
A) Should be applied everywhere
B) May not transfer — context matters; a pattern's success depends on the users, task, and situation, so validate rather than assume universal applicability
C) Is universally correct
D) Should be avoided elsewhere

**6.** Designing complex state (e.g., a document with unsaved changes, sync conflicts):
A) Ignore it
B) Is core interaction design — users need to understand system state (saved, syncing, offline, conflicted) and what their options are, or they lose trust and data
C) Is the engineer's design
D) Never occurs

**7.** Optimistic UI (showing success before the server confirms):
A) Is always wrong
B) Can improve perceived responsiveness, but the design must handle the case where the action ultimately fails — a designer plans for both outcomes
C) Has no design implications
D) Is the engineer's decision alone

**8.** Designing for latency and slow connections:
A) Assume everything is instant
B) Is real design work — skeleton screens, progressive loading, and meaningful feedback keep the experience usable when things are slow
C) Is the engineer's problem
D) Never matters

**9.** Micro-interactions and motion at this level:
A) Maximize them
B) Are used with intent and restraint — reinforcing feedback, continuity, and hierarchy, while respecting performance and reduced-motion needs
C) Are decorative filler
D) Are the engineer's call

**10.** Designing notifications and interruptions:
A) Notify users of everything
B) Requires judgment — respecting attention, batching where sensible, and reserving interruption for what genuinely warrants it; notification overload drives users away
C) Is the PM's job
D) Never needs design

**11.** Undo versus confirmation:
A) Always confirm
B) Undo is often a better pattern than confirmation for reversible actions — it keeps flow fast while protecting users; confirmations suit truly destructive, irreversible actions
C) Never allow undo
D) They are the same

**12.** Designing search and filtering for a large dataset:
A) A single search box suffices always
B) Requires thinking about how users actually look for things — search, filters, sorting, empty results, and refinement — matched to the data and the user's mental model
C) Is unsolvable
D) Is purely visual

**13.** Accessibility in complex interactions:
A) Applies only to simple screens
B) Becomes more important as complexity grows — focus management, keyboard operability, and clear state communication must hold up in rich, dynamic interfaces
C) Is automatic
D) Is the engineer's job only

**14.** Designing an interface others will extend (a platform, a builder):
A) Design only for today's content
B) Requires designing for variability — unknown content lengths, quantities, and combinations — so the design holds up as it is populated and extended
C) Is impossible
D) Needs no special thought

## Section B — Research That Shapes Decisions (Q15–Q26)

**15.** Research at the Junior 3 level:
A) Executing tests someone else designed
B) Increasingly driving it — choosing the right method for the question, running it well, and translating findings into design decisions
C) Only for dedicated researchers
D) A waste of design time

**16.** Choosing a research method:
A) Always use the same one
B) Depends on the question — generative research (interviews, observation) to understand and explore; evaluative research (usability testing) to assess a design; quantitative to measure at scale
C) Is arbitrary
D) Is the researcher's job only

**17.** Generative versus evaluative research:
A) Are the same
B) Generative uncovers needs and shapes what to build; evaluative tests whether a design works — a Junior 3 knows which they need
C) Only evaluative matters
D) Only generative matters

**18.** Synthesizing research findings:
A) List every observation raw
B) Is a skill — finding the patterns and insights across sessions, and translating them into actionable design implications, is where research creates value
C) Is unnecessary
D) Is the PM's job

**19.** A finding that contradicts a stakeholder's belief:
A) Suppress it to avoid conflict
B) Present it honestly with the evidence — research is valuable precisely when it challenges assumptions, and a designer advocates for what the evidence shows
C) Is invalid
D) Should be softened until it agrees

**20.** Bias in research:
A) Does not exist if you are careful
B) Is ever-present (leading questions, unrepresentative participants, confirmation bias) — a good researcher actively guards against it and is honest about limitations
C) Is acceptable
D) Only affects surveys

**21.** Recruiting the right participants:
A) Anyone will do
B) Matters greatly — testing with people unlike your actual users produces misleading results; participant selection shapes validity
C) Is the PM's job
D) Is irrelevant

**22.** Research as risk reduction:
A) Slows the team
B) De-risks decisions — learning a design is wrong from five users is far cheaper than learning it from a failed launch; research is an investment, not a tax
C) Is optional overhead
D) Replaces design

**23.** Continuous discovery:
A) Research once at the start
B) Is ongoing contact with users throughout development — a steady stream of small learnings beats a single big study, keeping design grounded as it evolves
C) Is only for new products
D) Wastes time

**24.** Combining qualitative and quantitative signals:
A) Use only one
B) Is powerful — quantitative shows *what* is happening (drop-off, usage), qualitative reveals *why*; together they give a fuller picture than either alone
C) Is contradictory
D) Is impossible

**25.** Presenting research to influence decisions:
A) Dump raw data
B) Tell the story — the insight, the evidence, and the implication for design — so the team can act on it, not just receive numbers
C) Keep it to yourself
D) Only show what confirms the plan

**26.** When research is not feasible for a decision:
A) Never decide
B) Use judgment informed by prior research, principles, and analogous cases, and be explicit about the assumption and risk — not every decision can be tested, but the reasoning should be sound
C) Guess randomly
D) Always demand a full study

## Section C — Data-Informed Design (Q27–Q38)

**27.** Product analytics for a designer:
A) Are the PM's private tool
B) Are a core input — usage, funnels, drop-off, and engagement reveal how the design performs in reality and where users struggle
C) Replace all research
D) Are irrelevant to design

**28.** A funnel with a steep drop-off at one step:
A) Is a data curiosity
B) Points to a likely design or experience problem at that step — a signal to investigate and improve, combining the *what* (data) with the *why* (research)
C) Is the user's fault
D) Should be ignored

**29.** A/B testing design changes:
A) Is guessing
B) Measures the real impact of a change against a metric with actual users — powerful evidence, though it answers "which is better on this metric," not "why"
C) Replaces design judgment
D) Is the engineer's tool only

**30.** A design that tests worse on a metric than the current version:
A) Ship it anyway — it looks better
B) Is evidence to weigh — either revise, or understand why the metric moved and whether it is the right metric; data should inform, not be ignored
C) Means the test is wrong
D) Means metrics are useless

**31.** Optimizing purely for a single metric:
A) Is always correct
B) Is risky — optimizing one number (e.g., clicks) can harm the overall experience or other outcomes; designers consider the whole experience and multiple signals
C) Is the only valid approach
D) Is impossible

**32.** Vanity metrics versus meaningful metrics:
A) Are the same
B) Vanity metrics look good but do not reflect real value (raw pageviews); meaningful metrics tie to user and business outcomes — a designer distinguishes them
C) All metrics are vanity
D) All metrics are meaningful

**33.** Correlation versus causation in product data:
A) Are the same
B) Are different — a change coinciding with a metric shift does not prove it caused it; controlled experiments (A/B tests) establish causation, observational data does not
C) Correlation proves causation
D) Neither matters

**34.** Qualitative context for quantitative findings:
A) Is unnecessary
B) Explains the numbers — data shows users abandon a step, but watching users reveals *why*; the combination drives good design decisions
C) Contradicts the data
D) Is the researcher's job only

**35.** Designing measurement into a feature:
A) Is the analyst's job after launch
B) Is part of design — deciding what success looks like and what to measure, before launch, so you can learn whether the design worked
C) Is unnecessary
D) Slows design

**36.** A metric moving for reasons unrelated to your design:
A) Is impossible
B) Happens often — seasonality, other changes, and external factors confound metrics; interpret data carefully rather than claiming credit or blame naively
C) Means the metric is broken
D) Is always your design's effect

**37.** Balancing data with vision:
A) Data always overrides vision
B) Both matter — data grounds decisions and catches mistakes, but design also requires vision for things data cannot yet measure; the skill is combining them
C) Vision always overrides data
D) They cannot coexist

**38.** Using data ethically:
A) Any data use is fine
B) Requires care — respecting privacy, avoiding manipulative "dark patterns" that exploit users for metrics, and designing for genuine user benefit
C) Is the legal team's job only
D) Is irrelevant to designers

## Section D — Content Design and Communication (Q39–Q48)

**39.** Content design / UX writing:
A) Is filler the content team adds later
B) Is integral to the experience — the words in an interface (labels, buttons, errors, empty states, guidance) are often what makes it clear or confusing
C) Does not affect usability
D) Should be technical jargon

**40.** A confusing button label:
A) Is a minor cosmetic issue
B) Can break a flow — if users do not understand what an action does, clarity of wording is a real usability fix, not decoration
C) Is the engineer's problem
D) Never matters

**41.** Error message tone and content:
A) Should be technical and terse
B) Should be clear, human, and helpful — telling the user what happened and how to fix it, without blame or jargon
C) Should blame the user
D) Should be a code

**42.** Voice and tone in a product:
A) Are irrelevant
B) Shape how the product feels and builds trust — consistent, appropriate voice is part of the experience, and it shifts with context (an error is not written like a celebration)
C) Should be random
D) Are the marketing team's only concern

**43.** Writing for scannability:
A) Long paragraphs everywhere
B) Recognizes users scan rather than read — concise, front-loaded, well-structured text serves real reading behavior
C) Is dumbing down
D) Does not matter on screens

**44.** Placeholder and lorem-ipsum content in designs:
A) Is fine to ship with
B) Can hide real problems — designing with realistic content (long names, edge cases, real messages) reveals issues that fake content masks
C) Is more professional
D) Is required

**45.** Localization and content:
A) Is only translation
B) Affects design — text length, formats, and cultural context change across locales, and designs must accommodate real translated content
C) Is irrelevant to designers
D) Only matters for logos

**46.** Consistency of terminology across a product:
A) Does not matter
B) Reduces confusion — calling the same thing by different names in different places makes users unsure whether they are the same; consistent terms aid comprehension
C) Is the writer's job only
D) Limits creativity

**47.** Designing helpful empty and first-use states with content:
A) Leave them blank
B) Uses words and guidance to orient users and prompt their first action — turning a confusing void into a helpful starting point
C) Is unnecessary
D) Is the engineer's job

**48.** Collaboration with content specialists (where they exist):
A) Hand off and forget
B) Is a partnership — designer and content designer shape the experience together, since layout and words are deeply intertwined
C) Is adversarial
D) Is unnecessary

## Section E — Design Systems at Depth (Q49–Q60)

**49.** Contributing components to the design system:
A) Is only for system owners
B) Is expected of a Junior 3 — designing new components to the system's standards, with all states and documentation, strengthens the shared foundation
C) Is forbidden
D) Wastes time

**50.** Designing a component for reuse (not just one use):
A) Design it for the single screen you need
B) Consider the range of contexts it must serve — different content, sizes, and states — so it works everywhere it will be used, not just once
C) Is over-engineering
D) Is impossible

**51.** When to create a new component versus compose existing ones:
A) Always create new
B) Prefer composing existing components; create new ones only when a genuinely new need exists — proliferating near-duplicate components fragments the system
C) Never create new
D) Is arbitrary

**52.** Design tokens as a system foundation:
A) Are irrelevant
B) Are the named, shared values (color, spacing, type, radius) that enable consistency, theming, and design-code alignment — the atoms of the system
C) Are only code
D) Are decorative

**53.** Theming (light/dark, brands) via the system:
A) Requires redesigning everything
B) Is enabled by well-structured tokens — semantic tokens let the same components adapt to themes without redesign
C) Is impossible
D) Is the engineer's job alone

**54.** A component with inconsistent behavior across the product:
A) Is fine
B) Undermines the system's value — the point of a shared component is consistent behavior everywhere; divergence should be reconciled
C) Is unavoidable
D) Is the engineer's fault

**55.** Governance of a design system:
A) Anyone changes anything anytime
B) Needs some process — how components are proposed, reviewed, and evolved — so the system stays coherent rather than fragmenting; a Junior 3 works within it and contributes
C) Should have no rules
D) Is pure bureaucracy

**56.** Balancing system consistency with a feature's unique needs:
A) Always break the system for the feature
B) Prefer the system; deviate only with justification and, ideally, by extending the system — so one feature's needs do not fragment the whole
C) Never allow any deviation
D) Is the PM's call

**57.** Documentation of usage guidelines:
A) Is unnecessary
B) Is what makes a system usable by others — when and how to use each component, with dos and don'ts, so the system scales beyond its authors
C) Writes itself
D) Is the engineer's job

**58.** The system and accessibility:
A) Are unrelated
B) Are linked — building accessibility into shared components (contrast, focus, labels) means every use inherits it, scaling accessible design across the product
C) The system ignores accessibility
D) Accessibility is per-feature only

**59.** A design system as a product itself:
A) Is a static file
B) Is a living product with users (the designers and engineers who consume it) — it needs maintenance, feedback, and iteration like any product
C) Is finished once built
D) Has no users

**60.** The purpose of investing in the system:
A) To constrain designers
B) To deliver consistent quality faster by not re-solving solved problems — freeing designers to focus on the novel and high-value work
C) To reduce the design team
D) To showcase craft

## Section F — Complex Responsive and Platform Work (Q61–Q70)

**61.** Designing one feature across web, mobile web, and native apps:
A) One design fits all
B) Requires adapting to each platform's constraints and conventions while keeping the experience coherent — respecting platform norms where they matter
C) Is the engineer's job
D) Is identical everywhere

**62.** Responsive behavior between breakpoints:
A) Only design the breakpoints
B) Consider how the layout behaves at every width, not just at named breakpoints — content should reflow gracefully across the full range
C) Is the engineer's problem
D) Does not matter

**63.** Adaptive content priority:
A) Show identical content everywhere
B) Shift what is emphasized by context and screen — a small screen or a distracted mobile context demands sharper prioritization than a spacious desktop
C) Is impossible
D) Is purely visual

**64.** Designing for accessibility across devices:
A) Applies to one platform only
B) Spans all — touch target sizes, screen-reader support, keyboard operability, and contrast must hold across the devices and inputs users actually use
C) Is automatic
D) Is the engineer's job only

**65.** Platform conventions versus brand consistency:
A) Brand always overrides platform
B) Is a balance — honoring platform norms (so the app feels native) while maintaining brand coherence; ignoring platform conventions creates friction
C) Platform always overrides brand
D) They never conflict

**66.** Designing for varied real-world contexts (bright sun, one hand, poor connection):
A) Assume ideal conditions
B) Reflects reality — designing for actual contexts of use (glare, motion, interruptions, slow networks) makes the product usable where people really are
C) Is over-thinking
D) Is the engineer's concern

**67.** Internationalization in complex layouts:
A) Never an issue
B) Requires accommodating text expansion, different scripts, and right-to-left languages without breaking the layout — a real constraint at scale
C) Is the translator's job
D) Only affects text

**68.** Testing complex responsive designs:
A) Trust the design tool
B) Check on real devices and sizes — legibility, target sizes, reflow, and feel differ from the canvas, and real testing catches breakage
C) Is the QA team's job
D) Is unnecessary

**69.** Designing a data-dense interface responsively:
A) Shrink everything to fit small screens
B) Reprioritize and restructure for each context — showing what matters and providing access to the rest, rather than an unusable miniature of the desktop view
C) Is impossible on mobile
D) Needs no thought

**70.** A responsive design that breaks with real content:
A) Is the engineer's fault
B) Is a design gap — designing with realistic, variable content (long, short, empty, huge) reveals where a layout fails before it ships
C) Is unavoidable
D) Does not matter
