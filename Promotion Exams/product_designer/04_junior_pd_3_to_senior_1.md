# Promotion Exam: Junior Product Designer 3 → Senior Product Designer 1

**Track:** Convergence — Design Specialist → Generalist Product Designer
**Format:** 100 multiple-choice questions
**Time:** 165 minutes
**Passing score:** 89 / 100

**Scope and purpose.** This is the convergence exam for the product-design track. It is parallel in spirit to the engineering convergence exams, but the convergence is different: a product designer does *not* become a software engineer. Instead, a designer converging to Senior 1 broadens from a specialist who leans toward one part of the craft (visual, or UX/research, or interaction) into a **generalist product designer** who does research, interaction, visual, content, and systems work; thinks about product strategy and the business; and collaborates with engineering with enough technical literacy to design realistic, buildable solutions and influence how things get built. This exam confirms the candidate still owns deep design craft, then tests that breadth and the senior judgment, influence, and product thinking the role demands.

**A note to the candidate.** If you answer Section A comfortably but struggle with the research, product-thinking, or technical-collaboration sections, that is information, not failure. A designer who is excellent at visuals but cannot run research, connect design to business outcomes, or work fluently with engineers is not yet ready for Senior 1. Deliberate broadening beats passing by luck and then struggling in the role.

**A note to administrators.** Unlike the engineering tracks, this designer's senior and leadership ladder is design-specific: Senior Product Designer → Design Lead / Staff Designer → Design Manager, or the Principal Designer individual-contributor path. The shared engineering exams (system design, distributed systems, engineering management, Principal Engineer) do **not** apply to this track and should not be used for it. The designer's upper-ladder exams are separate instruments.

## Section A — Design Craft a Senior Must Still Own (Q1–Q15)

A) Add a tutorial to explain it

C) Blame the users
D) Ship it

**4.** Accessibility at senior level:
A) A junior concern to delegate

C) Automatic
D) The engineer's job

**5.** Reusing the design system versus reinventing:
A) Reinvent for originality
B) Reuse by default and extend deliberately — a senior keeps the product consistent and reserves novel design for where it genuinely adds value
C) Ignore the system
D) Always build custom

A) Fix each instance forever
B) Address the systemic cause — a pattern, a component, a guideline — so the class stops recurring; a senior thinks in systems, not just screens
C) Is unavoidable
D) Is the engineer's fault

**7.** Content and microcopy:
A) The writer's job, not the senior designer's
B) Part of the experience a senior owns — clear labels, errors, and empty states are often the difference between usable and confusing
C) Filler
D) Irrelevant

**8.** Designing all the states (empty, loading, error, edge, permission):
A) Only the happy path matters
B) Is baseline senior rigor — complete designs cover the states real use produces, or engineers improvise them inconsistently

**9.** Motion and micro-interactions:
A) Maximize for delight

**10.** Craft precision in files and specs:
A) Engineers will fix imprecision
B) Is a senior standard — precise spacing, tokens, states, and behavior remove ambiguity and model quality for the team
C) Stifles creativity
D) Wastes time

**11.** A senior reviewing a junior's work looks first at:
A) Whether it matches the senior's taste
B) Whether it solves the problem for the user and the business, holds up across states and sizes, and is consistent and accessible — then craft details
C) Color choices only

**12.** Prototyping to resolve a question:
A) Always build the highest fidelity
B) Match fidelity to the question — a senior prototypes efficiently to test flows, interactions, or feel, without over-building
C) Never prototype

**13.** Responsive and cross-platform design:
A) One layout everywhere
B) A senior designs across sizes and platforms, respecting conventions and real contexts of use, and tests on real devices
C) The engineer's job
D) Desktop only

**14.** Iteration:
A) A sign of indecision
B) Where quality comes from — a senior explores, tests, and refines, and models this process for the team rather than defending first ideas
C) A waste of time
D) A junior activity

**15.** A senior designer's deepest craft responsibility is:
A) The prettiest screens

**39.** Scoping an MVP or first version:

**40.** Trade-offs between user needs, business goals, and constraints:

**41.** Designing for the whole user journey (not one feature):
