# Promotion Exam: Intern → Junior Product Designer 1

**Track:** Product Designer (Specialist — visual / interaction / UX foundations)
**Format:** 100 multiple-choice questions
**Time:** 120 minutes
**Passing score:** 89 / 100

**Scope.** This exam evaluates whether an intern is ready to be hired as a Junior Product Designer 1. Product design is not decoration — it is problem-solving in service of both the people who use the product and the business that builds it. This exam blends the craft foundation (visual design principles, typography, color, layout, and the design tool) with the thinking foundation (what design is for, common interaction patterns, accessibility basics) and the collaboration a junior must show. The bar is: "can be trusted with a small, scoped design task under supervision, will produce clean and considered work, and understands that design serves users and goals rather than personal taste."

**A note on the discipline.** Unlike the engineering tracks in this ladder, a product designer does not converge into a software engineer. The designer's path runs through its own senior and leadership rungs. What the designer *does* share with engineers is a product: they must collaborate closely, understand feasibility, and speak enough of each other's language to build well together. This exam begins building that bridge.

B) Is wasted effort — understanding the real problem before designing the solution is foundational

**11.** A designer's most valuable trait early on is:

**53.** Reinventing a standard control (e.g., a custom date picker or dropdown):

**55.** Error states and messages:

C) Should blame the user
D) Should be technical codes

**56.** Empty states (a screen with no content yet):
A) Should be blank
B) Are a design opportunity — a good empty state explains what will appear and guides the user to their first action, rather than leaving a confusing void
C) Do not need design
D) Should show an error

**57.** Loading states:
A) Can be ignored
B) Matter to the experience — feedback that something is happening (and how long it may take) prevents users from thinking the product is broken
C) Are the engineer's decision alone
D) Should be hidden

**58.** Feedback for user actions (a tap, a submit):
A) Is unnecessary
B) Is essential — users need confirmation that their action registered and what resulted; silence creates doubt and repeated actions
C) Slows the interface
D) Is decorative

**59.** Affordance in interaction design:
A) Is a pricing term

D) Means adding more borders

**60.** Consistency of interaction patterns across a product:
A) Is boring
B) Lets users transfer what they learned in one place to another — inconsistent patterns force relearning and erode trust
C) Limits creativity
D) Only matters visually

**61.** Progressive disclosure:
A) Shows everything at once
B) Reveals complexity gradually — showing what is needed now and deferring advanced options — so users are not overwhelmed
C) Hides critical actions
D) Is bad practice

**62.** Designing the "unhappy path" (errors, interruptions, no connectivity):
A) Is edge-case work of little value
B) Reflects real usage — things go wrong, and how the design handles failure shapes trust as much as the happy path
C) Is the engineer's job
D) Never happens

**63.** A confirmation for a destructive action (delete, cancel):
A) Is annoying and should be removed
B) Protects users from costly mistakes — irreversible actions warrant a clear confirmation, though not every action needs one (confirmation fatigue is real)
C) Is required for every click

**64.** Designing for the first-time user versus the experienced user:
A) Only design for one
