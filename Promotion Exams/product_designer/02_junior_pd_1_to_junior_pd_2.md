# Promotion Exam: Junior Product Designer 1 → Junior Product Designer 2

**Track:** Product Designer (Specialist — visual / interaction / UX foundations)
**Format:** 100 multiple-choice questions
**Time:** 135 minutes
**Passing score:** 89 / 100

**Scope.** A Junior Product Designer 1 handles scoped design tasks under supervision. A Junior Product Designer 2 should own the design of a small-to-medium feature end-to-end with light review — understanding the problem, exploring and prototyping solutions, applying and contributing to the design system, designing responsively and accessibly, running lightweight research, and handing off cleanly to engineering. This exam deepens interaction and information design, introduces real user research and prototyping, covers design systems and responsive design, and tests the collaboration a Junior 2 must demonstrate.

---

## Section A — Owning a Feature's Design (Q1–Q12)

**1.** Given a feature to design, the first step is:
A) Open the design tool and start drawing screens
B) Understand the problem, the user, the goals, the constraints, and how success will be measured — before designing solutions
C) Copy a competitor
D) Ask what colors to use

**2.** Framing the problem before solutioning:
A) Wastes time
B) Prevents polished solutions to the wrong problem — a clear problem statement is the foundation of good design work
C) Is the PM's job only
D) Is unnecessary for small features

**3.** A user flow:
A) Is a color palette
B) Maps the steps a user takes to accomplish a goal across screens — designing the flow before the screens prevents dead ends and gaps
C) Is a single screen
D) Is the engineer's diagram

**4.** Designing screens without mapping the flow first:
A) Is more efficient
B) Risks missing states, transitions, and edge cases — the flow reveals what screens and states are actually needed
C) Is best practice
D) Is required

**5.** Scope creep in a feature design:
A) Is always good — more is better
B) Should be managed — designing endless additions delays delivery and dilutes focus; a Junior 2 flags scope growth rather than silently absorbing it
C) Is the designer's job to encourage
D) Never happens

**6.** When requirements are ambiguous:
A) Guess and design
B) Clarify with the PM and stakeholders before investing in solutions — designing against a guess wastes effort and creates disputes
C) Design every interpretation
D) Skip the feature

**7.** Considering edge cases in a feature:
A) Is over-engineering
B) Is part of owning the design — long content, no content, errors, permissions, and slow networks all need designed states, not just the ideal case
C) Is the engineer's job
D) Is unnecessary

**8.** A "designed" feature that only covers the happy path:
A) Is complete
B) Is incomplete — real features need empty, loading, error, and edge states, or engineers will improvise them inconsistently
C) Is best practice
D) Is the QA team's problem

**9.** Estimating design effort for a feature:
A) Promise the fastest possible
B) Think through exploration, states, prototyping, research, and revision, and give a realistic range with assumptions stated
C) Match the engineer's estimate
D) Refuse to estimate

**10.** Reusing existing product patterns for a new feature:
A) Is lazy and uncreative
B) Is disciplined — consistency lowers the user's learning cost and speeds delivery; reserve novelty for where it genuinely adds value
C) Should be avoided
D) Limits the designer

**11.** Designing a feature that conflicts with existing product patterns:
A) Is fine — this feature is special
B) Should be reconciled — inconsistency confuses users; either follow the pattern or make a deliberate, justified case for changing it product-wide
C) Is best practice
D) Is the engineer's call

**12.** The measure of a well-designed feature:
A) How beautiful the screens are
B) Whether users can accomplish the goal easily and whether it moves the intended outcome — craft serves that, it is not the sole measure
C) How many screens it has
D) How much the designer likes it

## Section B — Interaction and Information Design (Q13–Q26)

**13.** Information architecture (IA):
A) Is the visual style
B) Is how content and functionality are organized, structured, and labeled so users can find what they need — the skeleton beneath the interface
C) Is the color system
D) Is irrelevant to product design

**14.** A confusing navigation structure:
A) Is a visual problem
B) Is an information-architecture problem — if users cannot find things, no amount of visual polish fixes it; structure and labeling must be right first
C) Is the engineer's fault
D) Is unfixable

**15.** Card sorting or tree testing:
A) Are visual design tools
B) Are lightweight research methods for understanding and validating how users expect content to be organized and labeled
C) Are prototyping tools
D) Are irrelevant

**16.** Labels and microcopy in an interface:
A) Are the content team's job, not design
B) Are part of the design — clear, human labels and messages are often the difference between a usable and a confusing interface
C) Should be technical and precise over clear
D) Do not matter

**17.** A micro-interaction (e.g., a toggle animating, a button's pressed state):
A) Is pointless decoration
B) Provides feedback and delight — small, purposeful interaction details make an interface feel responsive and alive, when used with restraint
C) Should be maximized everywhere
D) Is the engineer's decision alone

**18.** Animation in an interface:
A) More is always better
B) Should be purposeful — guiding attention, showing relationships, and providing feedback — not gratuitous; excessive animation slows and distracts users
C) Is never appropriate
D) Is decorative only

**19.** Designing state transitions (e.g., from a list to a detail view):
A) Is unnecessary
B) Helps users maintain context and understand what changed — thoughtful transitions reduce disorientation
C) Is the engineer's job
D) Should be instant and jarring

**20.** A modal dialog:
A) Should be used for everything
B) Interrupts the user and should be reserved for focused tasks or important decisions — overusing modals is a common interaction anti-pattern
C) Is never appropriate
D) Is the same as a page

**21.** Designing for interruption and recovery (user leaves mid-task and returns):
A) Is an edge case to ignore
B) Reflects real use — preserving state, saving progress, and letting users resume are part of a considered design
C) Is the engineer's problem
D) Never happens

**22.** Default values and smart defaults in forms:
A) Should be blank always
B) Reduce user effort when chosen well — sensible defaults speed common cases, though they must be easy to change
C) Are manipulative
D) Do not matter

**23.** Inline validation versus validation only on submit:
A) Are equivalent
B) Inline validation (as the user completes a field) often helps users fix errors sooner, though it must be timed well to avoid nagging
C) Only submit-time matters
D) Validation is the engineer's design

**24.** Designing a complex data table or dashboard:
A) Show everything at once
B) Requires prioritizing what matters, using hierarchy and progressive disclosure so users are not overwhelmed by dense information
C) Is impossible to design well
D) Needs no design thought

**25.** Cognitive load:
A) Is irrelevant to design
B) Is the mental effort a design demands — reducing unnecessary load (clutter, choices, memory demands) is a central goal of interaction design
C) Should be maximized
D) Only affects new users

**26.** Consistency between similar interactions across the product:
A) Is limiting
B) Lets users transfer learning — the same action should work the same way everywhere, or users must relearn and lose trust
C) Is boring
D) Only matters visually

D) Replaces design

**28.** Qualitative versus quantitative research:

**29.** A usability test:
A) Asks users if they like the design
B) Observes users attempting real tasks to find where they struggle — revealing usability problems that opinions and intuition miss
C) Is a survey

**30.** Leading questions in research ("Don't you love this new feature?"):
A) Are efficient
B) Bias the answers and produce useless data — neutral, open questions yield honest, informative responses
C) Are best practice
D) Have no effect

**31.** How many users are needed to find most usability problems:
A) Hundreds, or it is invalid
B) A small number (often around five per round) surfaces most major usability issues, making lightweight, frequent testing practical
C) Exactly one
D) Testing is never worth it

**32.** What users *say* versus what they *do*:

B) Often differ — observed behavior is more reliable than stated preference, which is why watching users beats only asking them
C) Saying is more reliable

**33.** Analytics and behavioral data:

**34.** A/B testing a design change:
A) Is the engineer's concern only
B) Compares variants with real users to measure which performs better on a defined metric — a powerful way to validate design decisions with evidence
C) Is guessing
D) Replaces design thinking

**35.** Designing based on a single loud user's complaint:
A) Is the right response
B) Is risky — one voice may not represent the user base; weigh individual feedback against broader patterns and data
C) Is always correct
D) Should be ignored entirely

**36.** Research that contradicts your design:
A) Should be dismissed
B) Is valuable — it is cheaper to learn a design is wrong from research than from a failed launch; revise based on the evidence
C) Means the users are wrong
D) Should be hidden

**37.** Assumptions in a design:
A) Are unavoidable and fine to leave untested
B) Should be identified and, for the risky ones, validated — unexamined assumptions are where designs quietly fail
C) Are always correct
D) Do not exist in good design

**38.** Research as a continuous practice:
A) Is done once at the start
B) Runs throughout — understanding users before, testing during, and measuring after; design informed by ongoing learning beats one-time research
C) Is only for new products
D) Wastes time

**39.** A prototype's purpose:

**41.** Wireframes:
A) Are finished designs

**46.** Prototyping edge cases and error states:
A) Is unnecessary

**47.** Testing a prototype and finding a flow is confusing:
A) Means the users failed

**48.** Fidelity and stakeholder feedback:

**49.** Prototyping to communicate with engineers:
A) Is unnecessary — a spec suffices

C) Replaces the spec entirely
D) Confuses engineers

**50.** Iterating on a prototype based on testing:
A) Shows indecision
B) Is the point — prototypes exist to be tested and refined cheaply; iteration on the prototype is far cheaper than iteration on built software

**51.** A design system:
A) Is just a color palette
B) Is a shared library of reusable components, patterns, and guidelines that keeps a product consistent and speeds design and development
C) Is a single screen template

**52.** Using the design system's components:
A) Is limiting and should be avoided

C) Ignore the system entirely
D) Use a random external component

**54.** Design tokens (named values for color, spacing, type):
A) Are irrelevant to designers

**55.** A component's states in the system (default, hover, focus, disabled, error):

B) All must be defined — real components have many states, and omitting them pushes inconsistent decisions onto engineers
C) Are the engineer's job
D) Are decorative

**56.** Consistency versus flexibility in a design system:
A) Consistency should be absolute with no exceptions

C) Flexibility should be unlimited
D) They do not interact

**57.** Contributing to the design system:
A) Is only for senior designers
B) Is part of growing — proposing improvements, adding missing components consistently, and flagging gaps strengthens the shared foundation
C) Is forbidden for juniors
D) Wastes time

**58.** A design system that drifts from the actual product:
A) Is fine
B) Is a problem — when the system and the shipped product diverge, the system loses its value; keeping them aligned is ongoing work

**59.** The relationship between the design system and the engineering component library:
A) Are unrelated
B) Should mirror each other — when design tokens and components map to code components, consistency is enforced and handoff is smooth
C) Are the same file

**60.** Patterns versus components:
A) Are the same

C) Patterns are decorative
D) Only components matter

**61.** Documentation in a design system:
A) Is unnecessary
B) Is essential — guidelines on when and how to use components make the system usable by others, not just a pile of parts
C) Writes itself
D) Is the engineer's job

**62.** A design system's ultimate purpose:
A) To restrict designers
B) To let the team deliver consistent, quality experiences faster by not re-solving the same problems — freeing designers for the novel and important work
C) To reduce headcount
D) To showcase the design team

**63.** Responsive design:
A) Is one fixed layout

**64.** Designing only at desktop size:
A) Is sufficient

**65.** Mobile-first thinking:
A) Means ignoring desktop

**66.** Content priority across screen sizes:
A) Is identical on all sizes

**67.** Touch versus pointer (mouse) input:

C) Only touch matters
D) Only pointer matters

**68.** Designing for platform conventions (iOS vs Android vs web):

C) Always override them with your brand
D) They are identical

**69.** Different device capabilities and contexts (camera, location, one-handed use, on the go):
A) Are irrelevant to design
B) Shape design — mobile users are often distracted, one-handed, or on poor connections, and good design accounts for real context of use
C) Are the engineer's concern only
D) Never vary

**70.** Text and layout expansion for different languages:
A) Never an issue
B) Is a real consideration — translated text can be much longer or shorter, and layouts must accommodate it without breaking
C) Is the translator's job

**71.** Designing dense information for small screens:
A) Cram it all in
B) Requires prioritization and progressive disclosure — showing what matters and letting users drill in, rather than shrinking everything unusably
C) Is impossible

**72.** A design that breaks at certain screen widths:
A) Is the engineer's fault
B) Is a design gap — considering the range of sizes and how the layout behaves between breakpoints is part of designing responsively
C) Is unavoidable
D) Does not matter

**73.** Testing designs on real devices:
A) Is unnecessary
B) Reveals real issues — legibility, target size, and feel differ from the design tool's canvas; checking on actual devices catches problems
C) Is the QA team's job
D) Is impossible

**74.** Orientation, safe areas, notches, and system UI:
A) Are irrelevant to designers
B) Are real device factors a designer accounts for — content must respect system bars, notches, and rotation to look right on actual hardware
C) Are the engineer's problem only
D) Never affect layout

**86.** Content and UX writing:

A) Is a distraction from design
