# Promotion Exam: Lead Engineer → Engineering Manager

**Format:** 100 multiple-choice questions
**Time:** 150 minutes
**Passing score:** 89 / 100

**Scope.** This exam evaluates readiness to leave the individual-contributor track and take on people management. The Lead role is still primarily about technical direction expressed through influence; the Engineering Manager role is primarily about people, process, delivery, and being accountable for outcomes the manager often does not personally produce. The questions are therefore largely scenario-based. There is more than one defensible answer for many items; choose the one a mature manager would pick as the **best first move**, not the only acceptable move.

**Administrator's note on answer distribution.** As with the previous exam in this series, the answer key is intentionally weighted. Healthy management almost never has a single "obviously correct" move, and candidates who pattern-match to the dramatic answer (fire them, escalate, reorg) are usually wrong. The key favors the calm, information-gathering, relationship-preserving first move. In debriefs, ask the candidate to defend their choice and to describe what they would do **next**, after the first move played out.

**5.** A common failure mode for new EMs is:
A) Talking too much
B) Continuing to do the IC work they were doing, leaving management work undone
C) Holding too few meetings
D) Being too strict

**6.** You used to ship features. Now your team ships features. You feel anxious because your individual output is low. The right interpretation is:
A) You are underperforming and should code more

C) You should ask to go back to IC

**7.** Time you spend coding as a new EM should generally be:
A) Zero
B) Limited and bounded — small things, prototypes, or load-balancing during crunch, never on the critical path

**8.** "Glue work" — onboarding, design reviews, mentorship, unblocking — was undervalued for you as an IC. As an EM your job is to:

**9.** A senior engineer on your team says they "don't want to be managed." The right response is:

**10.** Your skip-level (your manager's manager) skips a level and asks a question of one of your reports directly. You should:
A) Be offended and raise it
B) Be glad your report got exposure; ask both your manager and your report afterward what was discussed, in a curious not paranoid way
C) Tell your report not to answer

**11.** "Managing up" mostly means:
A) Flattering your boss
B) Making sure your manager has the information they need to support your team, with no surprises
C) Asking your boss what to do
D) Hiding bad news

**12.** A peer EM is struggling and their team's work is bleeding into yours. The right move is:
A) Let them fail so you look better
B) Talk to them directly, offer help, and only escalate if the impact on your team is unresolved
C) Escalate immediately
D) Reassign their work to your team without telling anyone

**13.** You no longer have time to be the deepest technical expert in every area. The right response is:
A) Pretend you still are

**14.** As an EM, the most important skill that is **not** technical is:
A) Public speaking
B) Listening

**15.** A pattern that signals you are growing into the role:
A) Your team makes good decisions when you are on vacation
