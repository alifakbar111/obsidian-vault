# Engineering Career Ladder — Promotion Exam System

A complete set of multiple-choice promotion exams covering every role in the ladder, from Intern to VP — across nine engineering disciplines plus a separate product-design track. This README is the map of the whole system; every exam below is an Obsidian wikilink you can follow.

---

## The model

Every engineer begins as a **specialist** (Intern → Junior 3), **converges into a generalist** at Senior 1, continues as a generalist through Lead, and then **branches** into either the management track or the individual-contributor track — both of which can ultimately reach VP.

```
        SPECIALIST STAGE               CONVERGE       GENERALIST STAGE          BRANCH
  Intern -> Junior 1 -> Junior 2 -> Junior 3 -> Senior 1 -> Senior 2 -> Lead -+-> Eng Manager -> VP
              (per specialist track)      (exam 04)                           |
                                                                              +-> Principal ---> VP
```

- **Specialist stage (Intern → Junior 3):** the engineer grows deep in one discipline. Exams are track-specific.
- **Convergence (Junior 3 → Senior 1, exam 04):** tests whether the specialist has broadened enough to operate as a generalist senior across the whole stack. The most important transition in the ladder.
- **Generalist stage (Senior 1 → Lead):** everyone is on the same path; exams are shared.
- **Branch (above Lead):** Engineering Manager → VP (management) OR Principal Engineer → VP (individual contributor).

Product Design is **not** an engineering track and does not follow this convergence — see its own section below.

---

## Format of every exam

- 100 multiple-choice questions, options A–D
- Passing score: **89 / 100**
- Sectioned by topic, with an answer key and administrator's notes at the end
- Answer keys are intentionally pattern-weighted (with deliberate breaks). The debrief — where the candidate defends their answers — is the real assessment, especially at senior and leadership levels.

## What the engineering specialist exams (01–04) cover per track

**Frontend** — HTML/CSS/JS/DOM and Git → React patterns, state, TypeScript, forms, API consumption, CSS architecture, Web Vitals, accessibility → convergence broadens into backend, databases, and systems.

**Backend** — programming, HTTP, SQL → REST design, auth, the runtime model, validation, testing → concurrency, database performance, caching, queues, observability, security depth → convergence broadens into frontend literacy and systems.

**Mobile** — language and runtime, app structure, UI/layout, lifecycle, networking → architecture (MVVM, unidirectional data flow), offline/sync, release engineering, notifications → convergence broadens into backend and web.

**QA** — testing craft with light scripting → balanced manual + automation → automation-heavy (frameworks, flakiness, CI/CD, API/performance) → convergence broadens into code literacy, reliability, and security.

**Security** — security mindset, vulnerability classes, crypto basics → threat modeling, web/API/network security, AppSec tooling → advanced vuln analysis, applied crypto, cloud/container security, detection, incident response → convergence broadens across the whole stack.

**Platform** — operational mindset, Linux, networking, scripting, cloud basics → IaC, containers, orchestration, CI/CD, observability → Kubernetes depth, reliability engineering, scaling, cost, platform-as-a-product → convergence broadens into the applications on the platform.

**Data Engineer** — data fundamentals, SQL, ETL/ELT basics, data modeling → reliable pipelines, warehouses/lakes, orchestration, data quality → distributed processing, streaming, performance/cost at scale, governance → convergence broadens into software engineering and the consumers of the data.

**Data Scientist** — analytical mindset, probability, statistics, experiments and causal caution, EDA → statistical inference, supervised ML foundations, honest evaluation, experiment design → advanced modeling, causal inference, experimentation depth, data at scale → convergence broadens into code literacy, production ML, and whole-system/business reasoning.

**AI/ML Engineer** — ML and software foundations, data for ML, evaluation, serving → engineering an ML component, training/evaluation craft, feature pipelines, MLOps foundations, applied LLMs → advanced/production ML systems, distributed training, advanced MLOps, RAG/fine-tuning/guardrails → convergence broadens beyond the model into services, the data platform, and business outcomes.

## The shared engineering exams (05–09)

- 05 · Senior 1 → Senior 2 — system design, distributed systems, reliability
- 06 · Senior 2 → Lead — architecture leadership, cross-team influence, technical strategy
- 07 · Lead → Engineering Manager — people and performance management, hiring, stakeholders
- 08 · Engineering Manager → VP — org design, executive partnership, the hardest people calls
- 09 · Lead → Principal Engineer — influence without authority, technical strategy, the senior-IC role

These apply to all nine engineering tracks. They do **not** apply to Product Design.

---

## The Product Designer track (non-engineering)

`product_designer/` is a **separate discipline**, structured differently on purpose. A product designer does not converge into a software engineer, so the shared engineering exams (05–09) do **not** apply and are deliberately absent. The specialize-then-converge shape still holds: designers often enter leaning toward one part of the craft (visual, UX/research, or interaction) and converge into a generalist product designer. The design leadership ladder above Senior Designer 1 is design-specific and not yet built.

## How to use this system — notes for administrators

1. **The score gates a conversation; it is not the whole verdict.** For technical specialist exams, a high score is a knowledge floor. For senior, leadership, and management exams, the debrief — where the candidate defends their answers and explains why the alternatives are wrong — matters more than the number.

2. **The convergence exam (04) is a gate, not a wall.** A specialist Junior 3 who scores 70–80 has done the depth work and now needs breadth. Coach them, give stretch assignments into adjacent areas, and re-sit later.

3. **Several convergence definitions need your decision.** For QA, Security, Platform, Data Engineer, Data Scientist, and AI/ML Engineer, "converging to Senior 1" means becoming a senior specialist-flavored generalist — not necessarily a product feature developer. Calibrate to what those Senior 1 roles actually do at your company. (Noted in each of those exams.)

4. **Have each track reviewed by a practitioner in that discipline.** Difficulty calibration is best validated by someone who lives in the discipline — especially the Data Scientist (statistical rigor), AI/ML Engineer (MLOps and LLM/GenAI specifics), and Product Designer (design craft, research, systems) tracks, which sit outside the software-engineering core.

5. **Product Design is structured differently on purpose.** It has no shared engineering exams, and its leadership ladder is not yet built. Do not substitute the engineering 05–09 for a designer's senior progression.

6. **Answer-key pattern.** Keys are intentionally pattern-weighted with deliberate breaks. For technical specialist exams, consider generating randomized-option versions (same questions, shuffled positions) to prevent answer-sharing across cycles.

7. **Platform note.** The `platform_engineer` track deliberately combines Infrastructure, SRE, DevOps, and System Engineer. If your company separates them, this ladder can be forked into role-specific variants. The same applies to splitting or merging Data Scientist and AI/ML Engineer.

---

*Built as a starting point and framework. Treat difficulty calibration as provisional until reviewed by practitioners in each discipline, and adapt to your company's actual stack, tools, and expectations.*
